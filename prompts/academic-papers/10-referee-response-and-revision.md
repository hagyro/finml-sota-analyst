# Prompt 10 — Referee Response & Revise-and-Resubmit Strategy

> **Purpose:** Turn a demanding referee report into a winning revision — a point-by-point response letter that is respectful, thorough, and strategically prioritized.
> **Use when:** You receive an R&R (revise and resubmit) or a reject-and-resubmit with a path forward.
> **Target standard:** 4* — every point is addressed, disagreements are handled with evidence not defensiveness, and the editor can see the paper improved.

---

## Prompt

```
ROLE: Act as a seasoned author responding to referees at a 4* journal, and as the editor
who will judge whether the revision earns acceptance. Be gracious, evidence-driven, and
strategic. Never dismissive, never capitulating to a wrong request.

CONTEXT
- Paper summary: {{one paragraph}}
- Editor's letter (priorities/tone): {{paste key lines}}
- Referee reports: {{paste, or list each point}}
- What I can realistically change: {{data/analysis constraints}}

TASK

1. DECODE THE VERDICT
   - Summarize what the editor actually wants (the 2–3 must-fix issues that gate
     acceptance) vs. the nice-to-haves. Referees write a lot; editors decide on a few.

2. POINT-BY-POINT TRIAGE
   For every referee comment, classify and plan:
   - AGREE & FIX: state the change and where it appears.
   - AGREE-IN-PART: what we do, and the reasonable limit of what we can do.
   - RESPECTFULLY DISAGREE: the evidence/logic for holding position, framed as
     "we tested this and here is what we found", never "the referee misunderstands".
   - OUT OF SCOPE: acknowledge, explain why, offer the smallest good-faith gesture.
   Present as a table: {Comment | Classification | Action | Where in paper}.

3. THE HARD COMMENTS
   - Identify the 2–3 comments most likely to sink the revision.
   - For each, draft the full response: what new analysis we ran, what it shows, and
     the exact text/table added. If a request is methodologically wrong, refute it
     with a citation-free, first-principles argument the editor can verify.

4. RESPONSE-LETTER DRAFT
   - Open with a genuine thank-you and a one-paragraph summary of major changes.
   - Then the point-by-point, quoting each comment in italics, our response beneath,
     and the revised text in quotes with page/line references.
   - Close by inviting further guidance. Professional, confident, not obsequious.

5. CONSISTENCY & OVER-PROMISING CHECK
   - Ensure every "we have added..." in the letter corresponds to a real change.
   - Flag anywhere the revision might create a NEW inconsistency elsewhere in the paper.

6. RISK ASSESSMENT
   - Which unaddressed or disagreed point is most likely to trigger a second-round
     rejection, and how do we de-risk it now?

CONSTRAINTS
- Address EVERY point; silence on a comment reads as evasion.
- Disagree only with evidence. Tone stays collegial even when the referee is wrong.
- Do not claim to have run analyses that were not actually run.
```

---

## Quality bar

- [ ] The editor's 2–3 gating issues are identified and prioritized.
- [ ] Every referee comment is classified and has a planned action.
- [ ] Disagreements are backed by evidence and framed collegially.
- [ ] Every "we added..." maps to a real change; no over-promising.
